# What Is Trust — Survey Note

This note is a cross-disciplinary survey of academic literature on the definition, multi-layered structure, and Japanese–English translation gaps surrounding "trust," conducted using LLMs (Claude / GPT) and compiled and verified by pjdhiro. It is not an original theory by pjdhiro but an organized review of publicly available literature.

---

## Summary

"Trust" is not a single concept but a composite one. Depending on the discipline, it is treated as an emotion, attitude, expectation, relational quality, institutional capital, behavioral strategy, epistemological condition, or experiential structure. The common core lies in "vulnerability," "expectation," "dependence," "unpredictability," "limits of monitoring," and "reparability." Between Japanese and English, the distinctions among trust / confidence / reliance / faith do not map one-to-one onto Japanese terms such as 信頼 (shinrai), 信用 (shin'yō), and 安心 (anshin).

## Multi-Layered Definition of "Trust"

| Layer | Trust treated as | Representative field | English term |
|---|---|---|---|
| Emotion | Affective bond involving felt safety, goodwill, and bodily relaxation | Clinical psychology, attachment theory | affective trust |
| Expectation | Anticipation that the other will act with goodwill and competence | Social psychology, organizational studies | expectation, belief |
| Relational quality | Stability and felt safety embedded in mutual interaction | Sociology, anthropology | relational trust |
| Institutional capital | Apparatus supporting social order and complexity reduction | Sociology, political science | institutional trust |
| Behavioral strategy | Choice to commit resources to another under uncertainty | Economics, neuroeconomics | trust behavior |
| Epistemological condition | Precondition enabling acceptance of another's testimony or information | Philosophy, clinical practice | epistemic trust |
| Technology calibration | Psychological state of adjusting dependence on automation / AI | HCI, AI research | calibrated trust |
| Experiential structure | Lived experience of "vigilance dissolving" or "being able to entrust oneself" | Neurophenomenology | lived trust |

## Japanese–English Translation Gaps

| English | Primary Japanese equivalent | Point of divergence |
|---|---|---|
| trust | 信頼 | Broadest term. Encompasses attitude, expectation, and relationship |
| confidence | 信頼, 確信 | Luhmann: confidence = dependence without alternatives; trust = commitment aware of risk |
| reliance | 依拠, 依存 | Philosophy: trust = reliance + awareness of betrayal possibility |
| faith | 信仰, 信念 | Conviction beyond evidence |
| trustworthiness | 信頼に値すること | Property of the trustee. Often conflated with trust (trustor side) |
| distrust | 不信 | Not mere absence of trust but active negative expectation |
| 信用 (shin'yō) | credit, reliability | Evaluation based on track record and competence |
| 安心 (anshin) | assurance, security | Yamagishi: elimination of uncertainty through institutional guarantee. Distinct from trust |

Yamagishi's (1998) core distinction: **Anshin** is a state where betrayal is impossible due to institutions or surveillance. **Trust** is expecting the other's goodwill while betrayal remains possible.

## Distinguishing Adjacent Concepts

| Concept pair | Key distinction |
|---|---|
| trust vs confidence | Confidence = dependence without alternatives; trust = active commitment aware of risk (Luhmann) |
| trust vs reliance | Reliance = functional dependence. Trust includes expectation of goodwill and awareness of betrayal possibility |
| trust vs trustworthiness | Trust is the trustor's attitude. Trustworthiness is the trustee's quality |
| trust vs distrust | Distrust is active negative expectation. Can coexist within the same relationship |

## Major Debates

1. **Emotion, belief, expectation, or relationship?** — The ontological category of trust varies by discipline. One must first determine "which level of phenomenon is at issue"
2. **Trust and risk** — Many disciplines assume uncertainty and vulnerability, but their meanings differ across fields
3. **Trust and vulnerability** — Since Baier (1986), the argument that trust is inseparable from vulnerability has been strong
4. **Trust, betrayal, and repair** — Betrayal is a damage unique to trust, qualitatively different from failure of mere reliance
5. **Trust and distrust** — Distrust is not the zero-point of trust. Low trust ≠ pathology
6. **Trust vs trustworthiness asymmetry** — Many measurement confusions arise from conflating these two
7. **Human trust and trust in AI** — Neither fully identical nor fully separate; a partial-overlap model is most appropriate

## Necessary Conditions for Trust

Five elements that repeatedly appear across domains as essential for trust to obtain.

| # | Necessary condition | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| N1 | **Vulnerability** | The trustor is in a state where they can suffer harm (Baier 1986; Mayer et al. 1995) |
| N2 | **Uncertainty** | The other's actions and intentions cannot be fully predicted (Luhmann 1979) |
| N3 | **Expectation** | An anticipation that the other will behave favorably in some way |
| N4 | **Dependence** | One's interests or well-being are entrusted to the other's actions |
| N5 | **Limits of monitoring** | The inability to fully monitor or control the other |

The five elements are logically linked: **Limits of monitoring** → uncertainty arises → expectations may be betrayed (vulnerability emerges) → dependence makes vulnerability real. Trust is thus "accepting vulnerability with expectation toward another on whom one must depend but cannot fully monitor," consistent with Rousseau et al.'s (1998) cross-domain definition.

Dependence without risk is classified as Luhmann's confidence or Yamagishi's anshin, distinguished from trust.

## Sufficient Conditions — What Must Be in Place for Trust to Obtain

Even when necessary conditions (N1–N5) are met, trust does not necessarily obtain. Sufficient conditions differ between cognitive and affective trust, the establishment mechanism differs between interpersonal and institutional trust, and thresholds vary by culture and history.

Sufficient condition candidates with high cross-domain commonality:

| # | Sufficient condition candidate | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| S1 | **Recognition of trustworthiness** | Judging the other to be worthy of trust (competence, goodwill, or integrity) |
| S2 | **Will to trust** | Active choice to accept vulnerability |
| S3 | **Relational history / context** | Repeated interaction shapes the depth and stability of trust |

When N1–N5 + S1 + S2 are all present, trust can be said to obtain in most domains. However, no single universal set of sufficient conditions exists.

## Common Structure

The skeleton of trust common to all domains: **Context** (uncertainty + limits of monitoring + dependence) → **Evaluation** (judgment of trustworthiness) → **Choice** (will to accept vulnerability) → **State** (acceptance of vulnerability with expectation) → **Updating** (strengthening, revision, or collapse of trust through experience).

Three common patterns:

1. **Trust is a three-place relation**: Not "A trusts B" but "A trusts B with respect to X." One may trust someone's competence but not their goodwill
2. **Trust and distrust can coexist**: Trust and distrust are independent dimensions. They can exist simultaneously toward the same object (Lewicki et al. 1998)
3. **Trust is a process**: Not a static asset but a dynamic process. Trust after repair is qualitatively different from original trust (reorganized trust)

## Conditions Under Which Trust Breaks Down

| Necessary condition | Corresponding breakdown condition |
|---|---|
| N1 Vulnerability | Exploitation of vulnerability → betrayal |
| N3 Expectation | Violation of expectation → expectations of competence, goodwill, or integrity are broken |
| N4 Dependence | Misuse of dependence → the dependency relationship is weaponized as power |
| N5 Limits of monitoring | Concealment / deception → being intentionally deceived in the unmonitorable space |

Depth of breakdown: **Surface** (a specific expectation was unmet → recoverable through explanation and compensation), **Middle** (decline in trustworthiness evaluation → requires substantive change + time), **Deep** (damage to "the capacity to trust" itself → requires clinical intervention and prolonged experiences of safety). Deep breakdown occurs through betrayal trauma, accumulation of institutional injustice, gaslighting, etc., resulting not in "not trusting the other" but in "becoming unable to trust at all."

### 5W1H

**What**: A cross-disciplinary survey of "What is trust?" based on academic literature. Covered more than 12 fields including psychology, sociology, philosophy, economics, neuroscience, HCI/AI, attachment theory, and neurophenomenology.

**Why**: Trust is one of the core concepts in Project Design Theory, and building a theoretical foundation with an eye toward connecting it to Kesson-Driven Thinking was necessary. The goal was to reveal the multi-layered nature that no single discipline's definition can capture.

**Who**: pjdhiro led the design and verification, integrating broad surveys from three engines: Claude (Codex), ChatGPT, and ChatGPT Pro.

**When**: Initiated on February 8, 2026 (conceptual foundations CN-005/006); broad surveys and nine deep dives were conducted intensively on March 26–27; the first phase was completed with the necessary and sufficient conditions report on March 31.

**Where**: Survey assets are accumulated in `knowledge/research/trust/` (18 files, approx. 340 KB). Conceptual foundations are in `knowledge/concepts/` CN-005 and CN-006. The public-facing content is this note (`content/knowledge/trust-en.md`).

**How**: A unified prompt (PROMPT.md) was submitted to three LLM engines to obtain independent broad reviews. These were consolidated into an integrated report, after which nine sub-themes (attachment, AI, neurophenomenology, repair, self-trust, gaslighting, experiential taxonomy, AI measurement, institutional repair) were explored individually. The final result confirmed the convergence of five necessary-condition elements and the domain-dependence of sufficient conditions.
