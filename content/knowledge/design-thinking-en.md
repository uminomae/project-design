# What Is Design Thinking — Survey Note

This note is a cross-disciplinary survey of academic literature on the definition, lineage, and hollowing-out of "design thinking," conducted with LLMs (Claude / GPT) and organized and verified by pjdhiro. It is not pjdhiro's original theory but a synthesis of publicly available literature.

---

## Summary

Design thinking is not a single methodology; at least three distinct layers coexist (Johansson-Sköldberg et al. 2013). Its core lies in the interplay of abduction, framing, and problem-setting — the five-stage process is an introductory recipe, not the essence. Design thinking is not the opposite of logical thinking; it is most accurately understood as a practice of hypothesis formation and problem redefinition that complements deduction and induction.

## The Three Layers of Design Thinking

Based on the distinction by Johansson-Sköldberg et al. 2013.

| Layer | Content | Key Figures | Period |
|---|---|---|---|
| Designerly thinking | Research on the cognition, practices, and modes of inquiry of designers. Design as a distinct domain of knowledge | Cross, Schön, Lawson, Rowe | 1970s-1990s |
| Stanford-IDEO method | Systematization of visual thinking, needfinding, and prototyping as teachable methods | McKim, Faste, Kelley, Brown | 1960s-2000s |
| Business design thinking | Innovation methodology packaged for organizational adoption | Brown (HBR), Martin, d.school Bootleg | 2000s- |

## Terminology

| Term | Definition | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Design thinking | Meaning shifts with context. Narrowly: the study of designers' modes of thought. Broadly: human-centered innovation methodology | Confusion arises when the three layers are not distinguished |
| Designerly ways of knowing | The concept of design as a distinct mode of knowledge (Cross 1982) | An epistemology of design, not a practical package |
| Human-centered design | A creative approach that starts with deep empathy for people and creates solutions aligned with their needs | A part of design thinking, not the whole |
| Abduction | An inferential operation that forms explanatory hypotheses (Peirce) | Abduction in design is not fully identical to hypothesis generation in science |
| Framing | The operation of reconstructing the frame itself (Dorst 2011) | A redefinition operation specific to design practice |
| Reflection-in-action | Reflection and experimentation in the midst of a situation (Schön 1983) | A description of reflective practice, not a celebration of intuition |

## Relationship to Logical Thinking

| Mode of Reasoning | Direction | Relationship to Design Thinking |
|---|---|---|
| Deduction | Known rules → specific conclusions | Used for defining technical requirements and verifying consistency |
| Induction | Specific cases → generalization | Hypothesis updating through user testing and observational data |
| Abduction | Surprise / dissonance → explanatory hypothesis | The core of design thinking |
| Design framing | Situation → reconstruction of the frame | A design-specific practice that extends abduction |

Design thinking is not the opposite of logical thinking but its complement. What it opposed was the narrow rationalism of "analysis alone suffices," not logic itself.

## Six Patterns of Hollowing-Out

| # | Pattern | What Is Lost |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Reduction to a five-stage process | The back-and-forth of framing; problem recomposition |
| 2 | Reduction to empathy workshops | Deep needfinding; long-term observation |
| 3 | Reduction to sticky notes and brainstorming | Judgment, synthesis, and quality of framing |
| 4 | Disconnection from implementation | Operations, evaluation, and organizational transformation |
| 5 | Innovation theater | Redistribution of authority, KPIs, and decision-making structures |
| 6 | Becoming an unfalsifiable buzzword | Conceptual boundaries; success/failure conditions |

## Effective Core and Lost Essence

| Effective Core (persists) | Lost Essence (disappears through hollowing-out) |
|---|---|
| Problem redefinition: recomposing what counts as the problem | Dialogue with materials: learning from the back-talk of sketches, models, and situations |
| Hypothesis formation: generative hypotheses about what does not yet exist but is desirable | Perceptual training: visual thinking, bodily externalization |
| Learning through prototyping: using prototypes as experimental apparatus for knowledge generation | Long-term judgment: context-dependent situated judgment |

## Key Debates

1. **Method, ability, or attitude?** — The d.school has advocated a shift from process to abilities. Kimbell organized three ways in which DT is narrated (2011).
2. **Are abduction and framing identical?** — Dorst positions framing as the design-specific extension of abduction, but framing involves value judgments and externalization, making it broader than Peircean abduction.
3. **Is hollowing-out an inherent defect or a distortion of the diffusion process?** — The split between designerly thinking and management DT (Johansson-Sköldberg et al.). Packaging has an unavoidable aspect in education and diffusion.
4. **Is HCD the whole or a part?** — IDEO frames HCD as a part of design thinking, but in the popularized version, HCD = DT tends to be consumed as equivalent.
5. **The scope of wicked problems** — Originally from social planning theory (Rittel & Webber). Not every design problem is wicked.
